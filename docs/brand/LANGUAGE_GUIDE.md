# Let the work carry the identity

I do not mind naming my roles when they help someone understand the work. What I dislike is turning them into a stack of self-assigned identities: `physician / educator / builder / speaker`.

In that form, the words stop providing context and start sounding like a claim about how I want to be seen. `Builder` and `speaker` are especially awkward because I would rather let the projects and talks establish that. The language is also generic. Almost anyone could swap in four flattering nouns and produce the same personal brand.

I want the site and LinkedIn profile to lead with the problem, the work already underway, and the invitation to join it—not a résumé compressed into a slogan.

## The rule

Use a title when it is factual and necessary for understanding the work. Otherwise, prefer verbs, projects, mechanisms, and evidence.

Keep:

- `I am a pediatric emergency physician, which means I spend a lot of time seeing what happens when care arrives late.`
- `I am helping develop KY-AHEAD, a University of Kentucky collaboration with Kentucky CHFS focused on cancer-screening gaps.`
- `I speak with health systems and public-sector groups about making AI useful in real workflows.`

Remove or rewrite:

- `Physician / Educator / Builder / Speaker`
- `Innovator · Thought leader · Change maker`
- any subtitle that summarizes character instead of describing work
- repeated credentials that are already obvious from the profile or Experience section

## Quick test

Ask:

1. Does this tell the reader what I am doing, or what I want them to think I am?
2. Could the same line appear on a thousand other professional profiles?
3. Would a concrete project or verb make the point more honestly?
4. Has the work already demonstrated the label?

If the line is mostly positioning, cut it. If the role gives necessary context, write it in a sentence and connect it to the work.
