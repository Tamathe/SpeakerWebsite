## July 28 — the shared-infrastructure idea

**Asset:** NBME clip beginning with `So let's do it for them` and ending with the intelligence-layer pitch.

There are a lot of very smart people building healthcare AI models.

That is not the part I lose sleep over.

I lose sleep over what happens after a model finds something important. Who receives the signal? Who calls the patient? Can the patient actually get there? And who notices when the whole thing quietly stops working?

At NBME NICE, I made a pitch that has been following me around ever since: what if healthcare AI were shared infrastructure instead of one more disconnected tool every hospital has to buy, connect, and maintain?

An intelligence layer over the data we already have could keep looking for useful signals—a missed screening, an A1C that needs follow-up, a patient falling through a crack—and connect each one to an actual next step.

The model is usually the easy part. The hard part is the system around it.

That is the work I want to keep pushing in Kentucky. If your group is wrestling with the same problem, bring me into the room. I would love to compare notes.

## August 4 — moving care upstream

Emergency departments are very good at taking care of the person who makes it through the door.

They are also excellent places to see what happens when every earlier opportunity has already been missed.

That is why I keep coming back to the phrase `move care upstream`.

It means finding the patient who is overdue for cancer screening before symptoms appear. Catching a vision-threatening problem close to home. Noticing the lab result that has been sitting there for a year. Then doing the much less glamorous work of making sure a real person gets a real next step.

AI may help us find the signal. It does not make the phone call, solve the transportation problem, build trust, or own the follow-up.

Useful healthcare AI has to include all of that boring human machinery. Especially that part.

If your team is trying to move care upstream, I would genuinely like to hear where the handoff breaks.

## August 18 — KY-AHEAD

Finding the patient is not the finish line.

It is barely the starting line.

KY-AHEAD is a University of Kentucky collaboration with Kentucky CHFS focused on a deceptively simple problem: too many Kentuckians are overdue for colorectal, cervical, or lung cancer screening.

Data and AI-assisted review may help identify who is due. But the useful part is what follows: outreach people can understand, navigation that fits real lives, scheduling, and a closed loop so nobody mistakes `we found them` for `they received care`.

That is why this work matters beyond cancer screening. It is a practical test of the operating model a larger health-intelligence layer would need.

One signal. One accountable next step. Then proof that the loop actually closed.

## August 21 — the eye as a wedge

The eye may be one of the most practical places to start building upstream care.

A retinal image can surface important ophthalmologic and cardiometabolic risk before a person feels sick. The camera can live much closer to everyday life—in a rural clinic, an FQHC, or potentially a retail pharmacy—than the specialist many patients cannot easily reach.

But the camera is not the point.

The point is whether the result reaches the right person, whether the patient understands it, whether the referral makes sense locally, and whether somebody helps close the loop.

That is why I think retinal screening is more than one interesting project. It is a useful wedge for the larger network Kentucky needs.

Start with a real signal. Connect it to real care. Learn what breaks. Build from there.

## August 28 or September 1 — the work before the label

People hear `health-intelligence layer` and understandably imagine software.

The first time I learned this problem at scale, AI was not the point.

At Mercy, I co-chaired a system-wide point-of-care ultrasound committee across a network of more than 40 hospitals. The ultrasound machines worked. The hard part was everything around them: workflow, equipment, credentialing, quality assurance, documentation, EMR integration, and getting different specialties to agree on what good looked like.

That experience has followed me into every AI project since.

A useful signal is only the beginning. The actual work is building the system that receives it, trusts it, acts on it, and learns when it fails.

So when I say the model is usually the easy part, I am not being philosophical. I have the meeting notes.

This is also why I think Kentucky can build something genuinely useful. We do not need one perfect tool. We need practical infrastructure that lets good signals travel all the way to care.

If your group has learned this lesson the hard way too, I would love to compare notes.
