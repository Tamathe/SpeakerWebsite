# Tama's LinkedIn Voice

Status: approved July 16, 2026

## The register

Write as if Tama is explaining an important problem to a smart colleague. The writing can be ambitious, but it should not perform ambition. Use concrete operating realities to make the larger institutional case.

Default to one or two substantive paragraphs when the idea fits. Start with the actual takeaway, explain the problem in plain language, and state what needs to exist or happen. Confidence should come from specificity rather than self-promotion.

Avoid manufactured hooks, rhetorical-question stacks, calls for engagement, hashtag language, résumé signaling, generic celebration, and conventional thought-leadership cadence.

## Reusable pattern

1. State the concrete institutional takeaway.
2. Explain where the data, work, authority, or constraint currently sits.
3. Show why no single organization can solve the problem alone.
4. Describe the needed capability through the services it must provide.
5. Correct important misunderstandings plainly, especially around governance, access, privacy, or ownership.
6. End with the real opportunity, decision, or work ahead. Do not add a social-media call to action unless it is genuinely needed.

## Approved example

The thing I took away from the General Assembly conversation is that Kentucky already has a lot of the data and clinical signals that could help us improve health outcomes. The problem is that they sit in different systems, under different organizations, and no one institution can connect and use all of them statewide on its own. If we want to identify risk earlier, understand what is happening across populations, and make sure patients actually receive follow-up care, the state, universities, health systems, payers, and community providers are going to have to build this together.

That means creating shared infrastructure for data access, interoperability, and analysis. I don't mean one giant database or unrestricted access to everyone's records. I mean a governed service layer that can securely connect existing systems, give each participant the information needed for their role, analyze signals across the network, and return something useful to the people caring for patients. We already know how to do pieces of this. The hard part—and I think the real opportunity for Kentucky—is building the infrastructure and relationships that let those pieces work across the state.
